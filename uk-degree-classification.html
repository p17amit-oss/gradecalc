The UK Degree Classification System

The United Kingdom uses a degree classification system for undergraduate honours degrees that is distinct from the US GPA model. Rather than a continuous numerical scale, UK degrees are awarded in discrete classifications: First Class Honours (a "First"), Upper Second Class Honours (a "2:1"), Lower Second Class Honours (a "2:2"), Third Class Honours (a "Third"), and, at some universities, an ordinary degree (pass without honours). This system dates to the 19th century and has remained largely unchanged in structure, though the distribution of awards has shifted significantly over recent decades.

Classification Boundaries

The boundaries are standardised across UK universities by the Quality Assurance Agency for Higher Education (QAA). A First requires 70% or above. A 2:1 (Upper Second) requires 60–69%. A 2:2 (Lower Second) requires 50–59%. A Third requires 40–49%. Below 40% typically means the student has not met the requirements for an honours degree. These percentages refer to the weighted aggregate of all counted assessments across the degree — not a single exam or year.

How Year Weighting Works

Most UK universities weight their years differently when calculating the final degree classification. A common structure is: Year 1 does not count toward the degree classification (but must be passed to progress). Year 2 contributes 30–40% of the final classification. Year 3 (final year) contributes 60–70% of the final classification. Some four-year programmes and integrated masters degrees apply different weighting structures. This means that final-year performance is disproportionately important — a student who struggles in Year 2 but excels in Year 3 can still achieve a First, while the reverse scenario is more damaging than students sometimes expect.

What Each Classification Means

A First Class Honours degree is the highest classification and signals exceptional academic achievement. Roughly 25–30% of UK graduates now receive a First, up from under 10% two decades ago. This trend — sometimes called degree classification inflation — has led some employers and graduate programs to look beyond the classification label to the actual institution and subject. A 2:1 from a Russell Group university in a demanding discipline may be viewed more favourably than a First from a less selective institution.

A 2:1 (Upper Second) is the standard strong-performance classification and is the baseline requirement for most UK graduate programs, professional qualifications (law conversion, PGCE, etc.), and competitive graduate employer schemes. It is the most commonly awarded classification. A 2:2 (Lower Second) still represents a real academic achievement and is accepted by many employers. A Third Class Honours degree is rarely discussed in graduate admission contexts but still represents a completed honours degree.

Comparing UK Degree Classes to US GPA

There is no official standard for converting UK degree classifications to US GPA. However, widely used informal mappings are: First Class Honours = 3.7–4.0 US GPA (A− to A). Upper Second (2:1) = 3.3–3.7 US GPA (B+ to A−). Lower Second (2:2) = 2.7–3.3 US GPA (B− to B+). Third Class = 2.0–2.7 US GPA (C to B−). These are rough approximations. A US graduate school admissions officer familiar with UK degrees will read your classification directly and apply their own contextual judgment rather than demanding a converted GPA figure.

What US and UK Graduate Schools Think of Each Class

For UK postgraduate programs (MSc, MA, LLM, PhD), a 2:1 is the standard minimum for entry. Many research councils and funding bodies also require 2:1 for scholarship eligibility. A First is often required for the most competitive funded PhD positions and Oxbridge master's applications. For US graduate programs, a UK First or 2:1 is generally viewed positively. US programs will ask for your degree class on the application and have internal conversion tables. A First is viewed as broadly equivalent to a 3.7–4.0 GPA applicant. A 2:1 is typically seen as equivalent to a 3.3–3.7 GPA applicant.

For International Students Applying with UK Degrees

If you hold or expect a UK honours degree and are applying to US graduate programs, submit your transcript as-is. Do not self-convert your classification. US programmes receive many UK applicants and know how to read a UK transcript. If you are asked to enter a GPA equivalent on an online application form, use the commonly accepted conversion (First = 3.7–4.0, 2:1 = 3.3–3.7, 2:2 = 2.7–3.3) and note that your grades are on the UK system.
