The UK degree classification system explained

The United Kingdom uses a classification system for undergraduate honours degrees that is entirely different from the US GPA system. Rather than a numerical average on a 4.0 scale, UK degrees are classified into honours bands: First Class Honours (colloquially "a First"), Upper Second Class Honours (a "2:1" pronounced "two-one"), Lower Second Class Honours (a "2:2"), Third Class Honours, and Ordinary (Pass) degrees without honours. Each band corresponds to a percentage range, though different universities set their boundaries slightly differently.

Most UK universities follow these approximate bands: First Class — 70% and above; Upper Second (2:1) — 60–69%; Lower Second (2:2) — 50–59%; Third Class — 40–49%; below 40% is typically a Fail or Ordinary pass. Some universities use slightly different cutoffs (for example, 68% at some institutions triggers consideration for a borderline First).

The key thing to understand about UK grading is that it is deliberately stricter than US grading. A 70% in a UK university exam is considered an excellent grade — equivalent to around 90–95% in a US context. UK professors rarely award marks in the 90s; a paper scoring 75 is typically considered outstanding. This compression of the marking scale is intentional and cultural, and it means that raw percentage comparisons between UK and US grades are not straightforward.

Why US graduate schools use GPA, not degree classes

US graduate programs use GPA (Grade Point Average) on a 4.0 scale as their standard academic metric because it allows direct comparison between all domestic applicants. When international students apply, admissions offices need to translate foreign grading systems into this framework. For UK degrees, they typically do this by mapping degree classifications to GPA bands, often using WES or their own institutional equivalency tables.

The absence of a direct numerical GPA from a UK transcript can actually be an advantage for UK applicants — admissions committees know that UK degrees are marked conservatively, and a First Class degree is understood to represent exceptional academic performance. However, it also means that the admissions committee must exercise judgment, which is why credential evaluations from WES are increasingly common for UK applicants to US and Canadian programs.

How US admissions committees view UK degrees

US admissions committees at research universities are generally familiar with the UK system, particularly at institutions that regularly receive UK applicants. A First Class degree from a Russell Group university (Oxford, Cambridge, Imperial, UCL, etc.) is understood as a very strong academic credential. A 2:1 is the standard "good degree" and is typically the minimum stated requirement for competitive programs.

Fewer US programs explicitly mention 2:2 as acceptable because it falls in a grey zone — the GPA equivalent (approximately 2.7–3.0) is borderline for many programs. Applicants with a 2:2 are advised to have very strong other components of their application (GRE scores, research experience, professional experience, strong references) to compensate.

Worked examples

First Class (72%): This maps to approximately 3.9–4.0 on the US scale. Most admissions committees treat a First as equivalent to a 4.0. Strong credential for all US graduate programs.

Upper Second / 2:1 (65%): Maps to approximately 3.3–3.7 (B+ to A- range). This is the most common UK degree class and is broadly accepted for US master's programs. For highly competitive programs, a 2:1 is typically the minimum expected.

Lower Second / 2:2 (55%): Maps to approximately 2.7–3.0 (B- to B range). Acceptable for some US programs with compensating factors; borderline for competitive programs.

Third Class (42%): Maps to approximately 2.0–2.3 (C range). Most US graduate programs require at least a 3.0 GPA equivalent, making a Third Class degree competitive at very few programs. Professional experience and strong references become critical.

What a 2:1 means for top US programs

A 2:1 from a top UK institution is a solid academic credential for US graduate admissions. MIT, Stanford, and Harvard graduate programs do receive UK applicants with 2:1 degrees — and some are admitted. However, at these ultra-selective programs, a First Class degree is strongly preferred. A 2:1 paired with excellent research output, high GRE scores, and outstanding recommendations from well-known supervisors can be competitive.

For mid-tier and highly regarded programs outside the top 10 — including many strong state flagship universities with excellent programs — a 2:1 from a good UK university is a comfortable fit. The combination of the UK's reputation for rigorous undergraduate education and the 2:1 classification is well understood by experienced admissions staff.

Limitations of this converter

This converter uses WES methodology and common US admissions practice to produce estimates. All results except First Class and Fail carry Moderate Confidence — the actual GPA equivalent depends on your specific institution, the specific examiner's grading approach, and the credential evaluator used. For official applications, obtain a WES evaluation.
