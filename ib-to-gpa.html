The International Baccalaureate programme explained

The International Baccalaureate (IB) Diploma Programme is a two-year pre-university curriculum designed for students aged 16–19, offered by over 5,000 schools in 159 countries. Founded in Geneva in 1968, the IB was created to provide a rigorous, internationally recognised qualification for students moving between countries and for those seeking admission to universities worldwide. Today, approximately 170,000 students complete the IB Diploma each year.

The IB Diploma requires students to study six subjects simultaneously — typically three at Higher Level (HL) and three at Standard Level (SL) — drawn from six subject groups: Studies in Language and Literature, Language Acquisition, Individuals and Societies, Sciences, Mathematics, and The Arts (or an additional subject from another group). In addition to six subject examinations, students must complete the Extended Essay (a 4,000-word independent research project), the Theory of Knowledge (TOK) course, and Creativity, Activity, Service (CAS) hours.

Why IB scores need conversion for US applications

US universities use GPA (Grade Point Average) on a 4.0 scale as their standard academic metric. The IB uses a total score on a 45-point scale, where each of the six subjects is marked 1–7, and up to 3 bonus points can be awarded for the Extended Essay and Theory of Knowledge combination. This means the maximum possible score is 45 (6 subjects × 7 points + 3 bonus points), and the minimum score to receive the diploma is typically 24 with certain subject minimums.

The conversion from IB total score to US GPA is not officially defined by the IB organisation or by any US standardised body — there is no single authoritative conversion table. The equivalencies used by this calculator are based on common practice in US admissions offices, community-reported outcomes, and equivalency guidance published by credential evaluation services. All results carry Moderate Confidence.

The IB scale: what 45 means and why it matters

A perfect score of 45 on the IB Diploma is extraordinarily rare — fewer than 300 students worldwide (out of approximately 170,000 diploma candidates) achieve this in any given year, representing roughly 0.15% of all diploma candidates. A score of 45 is unambiguously equivalent to a 4.0 GPA and represents top-1% academic performance globally. US universities, including the most selective, treat a 45 as the highest possible academic distinction.

Scores in the 40–44 range are also outstanding — a 40 represents approximately the top 5–10% of IB candidates. Scores in the 35–39 range are above average and competitive for strong universities, though not always for the most selective programs. The global average IB Diploma total score is approximately 30–31, which represents solid academic performance.

How US colleges view IB scores

US colleges view IB diplomas favourably as evidence of academic rigour and preparation. Admissions offices at research universities are generally familiar with the IB system and understand that an IB 7 in a Higher Level subject represents excellent achievement. The holistic nature of the IB Diploma — requiring breadth, an independent research project, and service commitments — aligns with many US universities' educational philosophies.

For undergraduate applications, many US universities also offer credit or placement for high IB scores, similar to AP credit. The threshold varies by institution — typically IB Higher Level scores of 5, 6, or 7 may qualify for credit in the corresponding subject area. Check with each individual university for their IB credit policy.

For graduate school applications, IB scores are a secondary consideration — graduate programs care most about undergraduate degree GPA and research experience. However, a strong IB background may be mentioned in personal statements as evidence of early academic rigour.

Worked examples at key score points

IB 45 (perfect score): Equivalent to 4.0 US GPA. Represents the top 0.1% of IB candidates globally. Competitive for all universities worldwide, including the most selective US programs.

IB 42: Equivalent to approximately 4.0 US GPA. Top 1–2% globally. Excellent for all US universities.

IB 38: Equivalent to approximately 3.7 (A-) US GPA. Top 10–15% globally. Competitive for most selective US programs; borderline for ultra-selective programs without other strong factors.

IB 34: Equivalent to approximately 3.3 (B+) US GPA. Above the global average. Competitive for strong universities but below the typical range for the most selective programs.

IB 30: Equivalent to approximately 3.0 (B) US GPA. Near the global average. Competitive for many universities; below average for highly selective programs.

Limitations of this conversion tool

This tool converts IB total scores to US GPA equivalents using banded approximations. The conversion is not linear — a difference of 1 point at the top of the scale (e.g., 44 vs. 45) carries different significance than a difference of 1 point in the middle (e.g., 30 vs. 31). Additionally, admissions committees may consider the mix of HL vs. SL subjects and scores on individual subjects, not just the total. A student with IB 38 from all HL subjects is typically viewed differently from one with the same total from primarily SL subjects.

Use this calculator as a planning and benchmarking tool, not as an official credential evaluation. For applications requiring an official GPA equivalent, consult a credential evaluation service.
