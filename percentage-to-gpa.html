Why there is no standard percentage to GPA conversion

The United States does not have a national standard for converting international percentages to its 4.0 GPA scale. This is not an oversight — it is deliberate. American universities developed the GPA system internally, and each institution applies its own methodology when evaluating international transcripts.

When you apply to a US graduate program, the admissions committee reads your Indian transcript as-is. They see your percentage or CGPA, compare it to other applicants from the same institution or region, and apply contextual judgment. They are not looking for a converted GPA from you.

Why two conversions are shown

Standard band mapping is what most US-facing grade conversion charts use. It divides the 0–100 scale into 10-point bands and assigns a GPA value to each. It is intuitive but has a serious flaw for Indian students: it was designed for American schools where 60% is a C and 70% is barely a C+. Indian grading is harder — a 75% in IIT Delhi is not the same academic performance as a 75% at an American state school.

WES iGPA methodology was developed specifically for Indian universities and accounts for this grading difficulty. Under WES, 60%+ from an Indian university maps to 3.7–4.0, not 2.3. This is why the WES number is typically higher than the standard band number for the same Indian percentage — and why it is the more widely accepted conversion for US and Canadian graduate applications.

How US graduate schools view Indian percentages

Most US graduate programs understand that Indian university grading is rigorous. A 70% from IIT or NIT is respected differently than 70% from a less selective school. Admissions committees at research universities routinely see Indian transcripts and have internal calibration. Context — your university ranking, your department's grading distribution — matters as much as the number itself.

For programs that require a credential evaluation, WES is the most widely accepted. Their iGPA conversion gives Indian students a more accurate picture of where they stand on a 4.0 scale relative to domestic US applicants.
