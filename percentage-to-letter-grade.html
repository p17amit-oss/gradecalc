Understanding the US Letter Grade System

The US letter grade system is the primary academic grading standard used at high schools, colleges, and universities across the United States. Unlike raw percentage scores, letter grades translate your performance into a categorical scale that allows comparison across different courses, professors, and institutions. The modern US system uses letter grades with plus and minus modifiers, giving 13 distinct grade levels from A+ down to F.

The Standard US Grading Scale

The most widely used scale divides scores into bands tied to letter grades and GPA points. At the top, 93% and above earns an A (4.0 GPA), while 90–92% earns an A− (3.7 GPA). In the B range: B+ covers 87–89% (3.3 GPA), B covers 83–86% (3.0 GPA), and B− covers 80–82% (2.7 GPA). The C range spans from C+ at 77–79% (2.3 GPA) through C at 73–76% (2.0 GPA) and C− at 70–72% (1.7 GPA). Below that, D+ runs 67–69% (1.3 GPA), D covers 63–66% (1.0 GPA), and D− reaches 60–62% (0.7 GPA). Anything below 60% is typically an F.

Plus/Minus Grading: What It Means

Not all institutions use plus/minus grading. Some award only A, B, C, D, and F — a simpler 5-point letter scale without the granularity of plus and minus modifiers. When a school uses plus/minus, the GPA calculation changes meaningfully: a B+ (3.3) versus a plain B (3.0) is a 0.3-point difference per course, which compounds significantly over multiple semesters. If your institution does not use plus/minus, your A is treated as 4.0 and your B as 3.0, regardless of whether you scored 80% or 89%.

Grade Inflation: Why the Same Letter Grade Differs by Institution

Grade inflation is a well-documented phenomenon in US higher education. Studies consistently show that A is now the most commonly awarded grade at four-year US colleges — a shift from decades past when a C was considered average. This means a B from one institution may represent different levels of mastery than a B from another. Graduate admissions committees at selective universities are aware of this and contextualise grades by institution reputation, major, and course difficulty. A 3.5 GPA from a school known for rigorous grading may carry more weight than a 3.8 from a school with more lenient standards.

What Each Letter Grade Means for Graduate School

If you are considering graduate school, letter grades carry particular significance. Most top-ranked US graduate programs (MBA, law, medical, engineering MS/PhD) expect a minimum undergraduate GPA of 3.0 (B average) just to be considered. Competitive programs at top schools typically admit students with 3.5+ GPAs. However, the minimum is not the average — many admitted students have GPAs well above the stated floor, particularly for funded PhD positions. A single course grade rarely defines your application, but a consistent pattern of C grades in core courses can be a significant concern.

Graduate admissions committees also look at grade trends. A student with a 2.7 GPA early in their undergraduate career who finished with straight As may be viewed more favourably than a student who maintained a flat 3.2 throughout. Demonstrating academic growth and mastery in relevant coursework matters.

Worked Examples

To illustrate: a student scoring 91% in Biology 101 earns an A− (3.7 GPA). In the same semester, they score 84% in Calculus (B, 3.0 GPA), 78% in English Composition (C+, 2.3 GPA), and 95% in Computer Science (A, 4.0 GPA). If each course is 3 credit hours, the weighted GPA calculation gives: (3.7 + 3.0 + 2.3 + 4.0) × 3 ÷ 12 = 3.25 for that semester.

Note how a 78% (C+) drags the semester GPA down despite strong performance in other courses. This illustrates why each course's letter grade matters, not just your overall average performance.

Difference Between Letter Grades and GPA

Letter grades are the categorical labels assigned per course. GPA (Grade Point Average) is the weighted numerical average of those letter grades across all courses, weighted by credit hours. While the two are directly related, they serve different purposes. Your transcript shows letter grades for each course. Your cumulative GPA is what graduate schools, scholarship committees, and employers see as a single summary statistic. Both matter: a transcript with mostly As in easy electives but Cs in core major courses tells a different story than a consistent 3.5 across all coursework.
