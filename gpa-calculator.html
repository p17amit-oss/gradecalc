How GPA is calculated in the US

The US GPA system assigns grade points to letter grades: A=4.0, A-=3.7, B+=3.3, B=3.0, B-=2.7, C+=2.3, C=2.0, C-=1.7, D+=1.3, D=1.0, F=0.0. Weighted GPA multiplies each course's grade points by its credit hours, sums them, and divides by total credit hours. This means a 3-credit course has three times more impact on your GPA than a 1-credit course.

Grade point scale reference

A+ is treated as 4.0 at most US universities (some use 4.3, but this is non-standard and not widely accepted). The range 3.7–4.0 is an A range, 3.0–3.6 is a B range, 2.0–2.9 is a C range, and below 2.0 means academic standing concerns at most programs.

Weighted vs unweighted GPA

Weighted GPA accounts for credit hours — a 4-credit calculus course carries more weight than a 1-credit PE elective. This is what official transcripts use. Unweighted GPA treats all courses equally, summing grade points and dividing by number of courses. This calculator shows both.

What GPA do you need for graduate school?

Top 10 US programs (MIT, Stanford, Carnegie Mellon, etc.) in competitive fields: typically 3.5+ GPA. Mid-tier programs (rank 20–50): 3.0–3.5. There is no universal minimum — strong GRE/TOEFL scores, research experience, and letters of recommendation can offset a lower GPA at many programs. CGPA (Cumulative Grade Point Average) is the Indian equivalent of cumulative GPA — the average of all grade points across all semesters and courses. Both are weighted averages, but they use different scales (CGPA typically 0–10 in India; GPA 0–4.0 in the US) and different grade point assignments. They cannot be directly compared without conversion.
